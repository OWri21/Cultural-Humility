First Nations Cultural Safety

Key Concepts

🌿
Country
A living, breathing entity
Country is not simply land — it is a living entity that encompasses land, waters, skies, culture, community, and identity. For First Nations peoples, Country is relational: you don't just live on Country, you live with it. Healthcare interactions must recognise this holistic relationship.
🤝
Cultural Safety
Determined by the patient
Cultural safety is not something a practitioner declares — it is determined by the patient. It means the patient feels safe, respected, and free from judgement. A culturally safe environment is one where people can share their needs without fear of bias, racism, or having their identity dismissed.
⭕
Holistic Health
Seven interconnected dimensions
Indigenous holistic health extends well beyond the biomedical model. It incorporates spiritual, emotional, family, community, cultural, and physical dimensions — all interconnected. As a dietitian, addressing nutrition without acknowledging these dimensions misses the whole person.
"Cultural safety is determined by the recipient of care."
The patient — not the clinician — decides whether an interaction is safe. Our role is to create the conditions where that safety is possible.

Factors affecting health engagement

  • Policies of child removal (Stolen Generations) created deep, intergenerational trauma and mistrust of government-run services.
  • Discriminatory hospital experiences and segregated care have left lasting impacts on willingness to engage with healthcare systems.
  • Understanding this history is not optional — it directly explains why a patient may disengage from care.

Asking about First Nations identity ensures access to appropriate, culturally safe services. However, the way you ask matters enormously:

  • Use a sensitive, private, and non-judgemental approach.
  • Explain why you are asking and how it helps connect the person to better support.
  • Never make assumptions based on appearance.

Stereotyping — even well-intentioned — degrades the quality of care and can deter patients from returning. Racism (both overt and systemic) directly influences health outcomes.

  • Examine your own assumptions before every interaction.
  • Avoid making food or lifestyle assumptions based on cultural background.
  • Call out racist behaviour when safe to do so.

In many First Nations communities, decisions are made collectively, not individually. Family members and Elders play key roles in health discussions.

  • Invite family to be present when the patient wishes.
  • Direct dietary advice to the family unit, not just the individual.
  • Recognise that "non-compliance" may reflect family food-sharing values, not disinterest in health.

Key support roles

Aboriginal Service Development Workers (ASDWs)
Cultural advocates who interpret worldview, bridge communication, and support patients throughout their healthcare journey. Their involvement can transform patient experience and outcomes.
Indigenous Liaison Officers (ILOs)
Connect First Nations patients and families to culturally appropriate services, navigate hospital systems, and act as trusted intermediaries between patients and clinical staff.
13YARN — Crisis Support Line
A 24/7 national crisis support line staffed by First Nations workers. If a patient is in distress, share this number: 13 92 76. Knowing it exists — and passing it on — is itself an act of cultural safety.

Cultural safety in clinical education

Clinical placements are where cultural safety moves from theory into practice. As a student dietitian, you will encounter First Nations patients in hospitals, community health centres, and remote settings. What you do — and don't do — in those moments matters.
🏥
On placement
  • Introduce yourself clearly — include your role as a student and ask how the patient would like to be addressed.
  • Never assume — don't make food, lifestyle, or health belief assumptions before listening.
  • Ask your supervising dietitian about local protocols for working with First Nations patients before beginning assessments.
  • If an ASDW or ILO is available, request their involvement early — not as a last resort.
  • Observe and reflect: notice how cultural safety is — or isn't — practised by the team around you.
📋
Documentation & care planning
  • Record cultural needs, food preferences, and family involvement respectfully and accurately in notes.
  • Avoid deficit language — describe what the patient can do and what supports them, not just barriers.
  • Flag cultural considerations clearly in handover and care plans so they aren't lost between staff.
  • Check whether the patient has identified as Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander in their record — if not recorded, ask sensitively and explain why it matters.
  • Dietary plans should incorporate traditional and preferred foods where clinically appropriate.
🗣️
Supervision & feedback
  • Debrief with your supervisor after culturally complex encounters — reflect on what went well and what you'd do differently.
  • Raise concerns if you witness culturally unsafe practice — speak to your supervisor or clinical educator.
  • Ask for feedback specifically on your cultural communication, not just clinical skills.
  • If you make a cultural misstep, acknowledge it genuinely, learn from it, and move forward — don't over-apologise or over-explain.
👥
Working with the clinical team
  • Advocate for the patient's cultural needs in multidisciplinary team meetings — you may be the only person who has spoken with them about food and culture.
  • If a colleague makes a stereotyped comment, address it calmly — "I'm not sure that's fair to assume" is a starting point.
  • Build relationships with ASDWs and ILOs as key members of the team, not optional add-ons.
  • Share what you learn about a patient's cultural context with the team (with consent) — cultural knowledge should not sit in silos.
💬
A note on discomfort: You will feel unsure, make mistakes, and sometimes not know what to do. This is normal and expected. Cultural safety is not about perfection — it is about staying curious, taking responsibility, and continuing to grow. The willingness to sit with discomfort and learn from it is itself a practice of humility.

Cultural Humility & Cultural Competence

Foundational concepts

🧠
Ontology & Epistemology
Why the way we know shapes the care we give
Ontology
The study of what exists — the nature of reality, being, and the world. Ontology asks: What is real? What kinds of things exist? How do they relate?
In health: does the body exist as a biological machine to be fixed, or as part of a web of relationships, spirit, land, and community? Both are ontological positions.
Epistemology
The study of how we know what we know — the nature, sources, and limits of knowledge. Epistemology asks: What counts as evidence? Whose knowledge is valid? How is knowledge produced?
In health: is randomised controlled trial evidence the only legitimate basis for dietary advice, or do lived experience, community knowledge, and ancestral practice also constitute valid knowing?
A real example — your educator
Ontology
What does your educator believe exists and is real?
Health is fundamentally relational and social, not merely biological. Food exists not just as nutrients but as culture, identity, memory, and connection. Place is real and meaningful — where knowledge comes from matters, not just what it says. This shows up in the inclusion of local Sunshine Coast and Brisbane/Meanjin-specific resources: Aunty Dale Chapman's shop, the Turrbal Our Story page, the Meanjin essay. These reflect a belief that situated, local knowledge is as real and as valuable as universal clinical evidence.
Epistemology
How does your educator believe we come to know things?
Knowledge is built through dialogue and questioning, not just transmission — which is exactly why this workshop uses a Socratic AI tutor rather than a quiz. Multiple sources count as valid knowledge: academic frameworks (Kleinman, Campinha-Bacote) sit alongside Elder voices, community organisations, and First Nations food businesses. Students learn best when they encounter real complexity — hence case studies with messy, culturally layered clients rather than clean textbook scenarios.
The key insight: Your educator's ontology (health is relational, place matters) directly shapes their epistemology (we learn through dialogue with real complexity, from multiple sources). The two cannot be separated. Now ask yourself — what are your ontological and epistemological assumptions, and how might they shape the kind of dietitian you become?
Why this matters for practice

Every healthcare interaction is shaped by assumptions about reality and knowledge — usually invisible ones. When a clinician and a patient hold different ontologies, they may be talking about fundamentally different things even when using the same words. Recognising this is the foundation of cultural humility.

Western Biomedical
First Nations
Ontology — what is the body?
A physical, biological system. Health = absence of disease. The individual body is the primary unit of care.
A relational, holistic entity. Health = balance across spiritual, emotional, physical, family, community, and cultural dimensions. The individual cannot be separated from Country, kin, and spirit.
Epistemology — what counts as knowledge?
Scientific method, peer-reviewed evidence, randomised controlled trials, reproducibility. Knowledge is universal and context-independent.
Ancestral knowledge, Elder wisdom, lived experience, story, Country, ceremony, and kinship. Knowledge is relational, contextual, and held collectively across generations.
Time and causality
Linear: cause precedes effect. Focus on present illness and future prognosis.
Circular and continuous: past, present, and future are interconnected. The Dreaming is not historical — it is ongoing and alive in the present.
Relationship to Country
Land as resource or environment. Health policy may acknowledge social determinants, but Country is rarely considered a health determinant.
Country is inseparable from health and identity. Displacement from Country is a direct cause of ill health. Connection to Country heals.
Key insight for students

Neither worldview is wrong — they are different ways of understanding a complex reality. The problem arises when one is treated as universal and the other is dismissed as unscientific. In clinical settings, the Western biomedical worldview is structurally dominant. Recognising this power imbalance is an act of cultural safety.

Ontological and epistemological differences show up directly in dietetic consultations — often without either party realising it.

🥘
Food as medicine vs food as culture
Biomedical epistemology classifies foods by macronutrients, glycaemic index, and clinical outcomes. First Nations and many CALD epistemologies understand food as carrying cultural memory, spiritual significance, and relational meaning. When a patient declines a "healthy" substitute for a traditional food, they may be asserting an ontological claim about what food actually is.
📊
What counts as evidence?
A patient may report that they feel well when eating particular traditional foods, or that certain foods are connected to ceremony and healing. Western clinical epistemology may not have an RCT to validate this — but that does not make the knowledge less real or less valid. Asking "why do you believe that?" is very different from asking "tell me more about what that food means to you."
👨‍👩‍👧‍👦
The individual vs the collective
Dietetics is typically framed around individual behaviour change — personal dietary goals, personal motivation, personal adherence. In many First Nations and collectivist cultural worldviews, food decisions are made relationally: what is cooked reflects obligations to family, community, and ceremony. Framing dietary advice as individual choice may be ontologically incompatible with how a patient understands their own agency.
🌿
Traditional & bush foods
Bush tucker — kangaroo, emu, witchetty grubs, bush tomatoes, native greens — carries profound ontological meaning. It is not simply an alternative protein source. It connects the patient to Country, kin, and ancestral knowledge. When incorporating traditional foods into a care plan, approach from curiosity about meaning and relationship, not just nutritional composition.
🗣️
Whose expertise counts?
In the consultation room, the dietitian holds institutional epistemic authority — the degree, the evidence base, the clinical role. But the patient holds deep expertise in their own body, their community's food knowledge, and what has worked across generations. Cultural humility requires genuinely redistributing epistemic authority in the consultation — listening to know, not just to respond.
⚠️
When worldviews conflict
A patient with type 2 diabetes may refuse to restrict certain foods because of their ceremonial role. A patient from a collectivist culture may not follow an individual meal plan because food sharing is a moral obligation. Rather than labelling this "non-compliance," ask: whose ontology is determining what counts as the "right" choice here? Start from the patient's worldview, then find points of connection.

Humility vs competence — what's the difference?

🪞
Cultural Humility
A lifelong journey, not a destination
Cultural humility is a process, not a product. It involves ongoing self-reflection, genuine curiosity about others' experiences, and a willingness to recognise and address power imbalances in the practitioner-patient relationship. It's about what you do and how you show up — indefinitely.
📐
Cultural Competence
A continuum, not an endpoint
Cultural competence is a continuum of knowledge, skills, attitudes, and awareness. Campinha-Bacote's ASKED model provides a practical framework. Importantly, you never "arrive" at full competence — the goal is to keep growing. Competence without humility risks becoming cultural arrogance.

Cultural self-awareness

🪞 Starting with yourself Kittler, Sucher & Nahikian-Nelms, 2017

"You cannot work cross-culturally unless you first understand your own cultural lens. Without this, we automatically universalise our own worldview — a form of ethnocentrism."

  • Every practitioner belongs to a culture — including the culture of biomedicine, which has its own unexamined assumptions about bodies, food, and health.
  • Clients may hold spiritual, relational, ancestral, or community-based explanations of health that are equally valid — and often more meaningful to them than a clinical diagnosis.
  • Expect to feel discomfort when your worldview is challenged. That discomfort is the signal that learning is happening.
  • Self-awareness does not make you neutral. It makes you less likely to mistake your perspective for a universal truth.
Reflective prompt: What cultural messages shaped your relationship with food growing up? How might those assumptions show up in a consultation with someone whose food world looks very different from yours?

Culturally sensitive interviewing — Kleinman's questions

Understanding health through the client's narrative Kleinman & Benson, PLoS Med, 2006

Rather than imposing a biomedical frame, Kleinman's questions invite the client to explain their own understanding of their health, food, and illness. Click each question to see how to use it in a dietetic consultation.

1 "What do you think is going on with your health?" ▶
Opens space for the client's own explanatory model — their cultural, spiritual, or experiential understanding of their condition. You may learn things a medical history never would.
2 "Why do you think this problem started?" ▶
Reveals causal beliefs — stress, spiritual imbalance, food "wrongness", ancestral factors. Knowing the client's causal model shapes how you frame advice so it can actually be heard.
3 "How do you manage it?" ▶
Uncovers existing food, herbal, or traditional practices — which may be supportive, neutral, or occasionally in tension with clinical goals. Never dismiss without first understanding.
4 "Who helps you with health or food decisions?" ▶
Identifies decision-making networks — often family, Elders, community figures, or religious leaders. In many cultures, individual "patient autonomy" is less relevant than collective family decision-making. Knowing this is essential for building a plan that will actually work.
5 "What treatments or foods do you believe help?" ▶
Positions the client as the expert in their own health. Helps you identify protective foods and practices worth affirming — and opens a genuinely collaborative space for negotiation where worldviews differ.

Cultural communication in practice

  • Ask "What does a typical day of eating look like for you?" rather than assuming based on background.
  • Avoid questions that presuppose Western food norms (e.g. "Do you eat three meals a day?").
  • Invite the patient to teach you about their food traditions — curiosity, not assessment, is the posture.
  • Avoid jargon — "saturated fats" becomes "fats that are solid at room temperature", "glycaemic control" becomes "keeping blood sugar steady". Speak in language that doesn't require a health degree.
  • Avoid idioms — phrases like "cut back", "rule of thumb", or "watch what you eat" may not translate meaningfully, literally or culturally.
  • Supplement verbal information with culturally appropriate visuals — food models, pictures, gestures, and locally familiar foods rather than generic Western food guides.
  • Speak in short sentences and pause frequently; don't front-load a consultation with information.
  • Use the client's language where possible — even just greetings or basic food words signal respect and willingness.
  • Check health literacy without assuming deficit — ask "Can you show me on this label what you would eat?"
  • Use teach-back: ask the client to repeat back key information in their own words — not to test them, but to check that your explanation landed as intended.
  • Understand that food is deeply cultural — linked to identity, ceremony, and community belonging.
  • Never dismiss traditional foods; explore how they can be incorporated into nutritional plans.
  • Recognise food sharing as a cultural obligation — a "non-compliant diet" may reflect cultural values in action.

Dietetic practice must recognise the cultural significance of foods — not just their nutrient profile. We are not only analysing intake; we're analysing meaning. (Kittler, Sucher & Nahikian-Nelms, 2017)

  • Foods used for healing — many cultures have specific foods believed to restore health, warmth, or balance. These are not superstitions; they are knowledge systems. Ask about them.
  • Foods that are culturally avoided — avoidances may be religious, spiritual, seasonal, or related to life stage (e.g. post-partum, illness, mourning). Never assume a "restriction" is simply preference.
  • Ethnic foods and frequency — staple foods, preparation methods, and eating patterns may differ substantially from population averages used in standard food frequency questionnaires.
  • Household decision-making around food — who shops, who cooks, who decides what is eaten. Individual dietary advice may be ineffective if it ignores household dynamics.
  • Ritual and festival foods — celebratory or ceremonial eating is often nutritionally dense or structurally different from everyday eating. Context matters before making judgements about "usual intake".
  • Fasting seasons and practices — many religions and cultures involve fasting (Ramadan, Lent, Indigenous ceremonial fasting). Dietary plans must accommodate — not override — these practices.

Cultural values are not background noise — they shape every food decision a client makes. Being aware of these domains helps you ask better questions and design more workable plans.

  • Family structure — nuclear, extended, multigenerational households each have different food procurement, preparation, and decision-making dynamics.
  • Gender roles — who is responsible for cooking, shopping, and making health decisions may be culturally defined in ways that affect how you engage with the patient.
  • Age and respect for Elders — in many cultures, Elders hold significant authority over health and food decisions. Engaging the family system, not just the individual, may be essential.
  • Religious beliefs and food rules — halal, kosher, vegetarianism, fasting observances, and food blessings all shape intake. Know what to ask and how to ask it with respect.
  • Cultural beliefs about wellness and illness — illness may be understood as spiritual, relational, or energetic (e.g. "hot/cold" food theories in many Asian and Latin American traditions). These explanatory models are meaningful and clinically relevant.
  • Food preparation customs — cooking methods, timing, spices, and communal eating styles affect nutrient retention, portion sizes, and the social meaning of food.
  • Acculturation level — clients who have recently migrated may eat very differently from long-settled community members. Acculturation affects food access, food identity, and comfort with biomedical encounters.

Further tips — assessing culturally diverse patients

Useful questions for cultural assessment Kittler et al.

The following questions help open respectful conversations about cultural beliefs, practices, and health experiences. Use them to supplement your assessment — not as a checklist to rush through.

  • "So that I might be aware of and respect your cultural beliefs…" — use this as an opening framing phrase before asking any sensitive cultural questions.
  • "Can you tell me what languages are spoken in your home and the languages that you understand and speak?"
  • "Please describe your usual diet. Also, are there times during the year when you change your diet in celebration of religious and other ethnic holidays?"
  • "Can you tell me about beliefs and practices including special events such as birth, marriage and death that you feel I should know?"
  • "Can you tell me about your experiences with health care providers in your native country? How often each year did you see a health care provider before you arrived in ______? Have you noticed any differences between the type of care you received in your native country and the type you receive here? If yes, could you tell me about those differences?"
Remember: Always introduce these questions with a phrase like the first one above — signalling your intent to learn and respect, not to judge or categorise, sets the tone for the whole consultation.

Working with interpreters

Key principles for interpreter-assisted consultations Practice skills
  • Speak directly to the client, not to the interpreter — maintain eye contact with the patient.
  • Avoid idioms, metaphors, and culturally dependent phrases (e.g. "eat like a horse").
  • Plan sessions carefully — allow significantly more time than usual.
  • Check comprehension using back-translation ("Could you tell me in your own words what I've just explained?").
  • Brief the interpreter beforehand — share the purpose of the session and key terminology.
  • Use professional interpreters, not family members, for clinical consultations where possible.

An Australian qualitative study using focus groups with CALD inpatients from Asian, Mediterranean, Arabic, and Indian Subcontinent communities. Three major themes emerged: familiar food as a source of comfort in an unfamiliar environment; cultural food traditions as wisdom about health and healing; and culturally appropriate food as physical support for recovery. The hospital menu studied was largely Anglo-European and not available in languages other than English. Directly relevant to dietetic practice with CALD patients.

Case 1 — Rahma
📄 Case brief▼
Rahma is a 42-year-old Hazara woman from Afghanistan, arrived in Australia 2 years ago. Limited English; 14-year-old daughter present and attempting to translate.

Background: History of gestational diabetes, now borderline T2DM. Traditional diet (rice, naan, lentils, yogurt). Irregular meal patterns due to factory shifts. Distrust of unfamiliar health systems.

Tasks: Request a professional female interpreter and explain why family cannot interpret high-risk health information. Adapt diabetes education to plain language. Replace idioms with clear instructions. Use visuals for blood sugar patterns and portions. Apply Kleinman-style questions to understand Rahma's beliefs about diabetes.

Case 2 — Mr. Gupta
📄 Case brief▼
Mr. Gupta is a 63-year-old man recently diagnosed with hyperlipidaemia. Lives with his adult son and daughter-in-law.

Background: Strong preference for traditional Gujarati vegetarian food (fried snacks, ghee-based dishes). Eldest son manages health appointments; daughter-in-law cooks all meals. Believes strength comes from "heavy, warm foods." Resistant to Western dietary advice.

Tasks: Map the family decision-making structure using Campinha-Bacote's encounters and cultural knowledge. Apply Acknowledge–Recommend–Negotiate from the LEARN model. Offer culturally relevant alternatives. Identify how hot/cold beliefs influence food choices.

Case 3 — Mr. Rossi
📄 Case brief▼
Mr. Rossi is a 79-year-old Italian-born widower with limited reading ability in English and Italian. Uncontrolled hypertension and high sodium intake.

Background: Diet of preserved meats, cheeses, homemade pickled vegetables. Deep emotional attachment to foods prepared by his late wife. Hides difficulty reading labels. Feels judged by previous clinicians.

Tasks: Conduct a nutrition assessment using open-ended, non-literacy-dependent questions. Use visual tools (food models, pictures of lower-salt alternatives). Explore food's emotional meaning using Kleinman's approach. Ask what family traditions matter most before recommending changes.

Case 4 — Sione
📄 Case brief▼
Sione is a 19-year-old Tongan-Australian university student and rugby player.

Background: Eats high-energy traditional family meals at home (large portions, shared dishes). Team coach recommended "clean eating," which conflicts with family values. Feels guilt refusing food from aunties. Worried that health professionals don't understand Pacific food culture.

Tasks: Explore the meaning of food in Sione's family — community, respect, identity. Identify where Western nutrition guidance clashes with cultural expectations. Negotiate realistic performance nutrition goals that honour cultural meals. Practise cultural humility: acknowledge what you don't know about Pacific foodways.
